Uncloaking Hidden Repeating Fast Radio Bursts With Unsupervised Machine Learning

Bo Han Chen et. al. (2021)

Introduction

Meaning

‘Hidden Repeating Fast Radio Bursts’

  • Fast Radio Bursts (FRB) are known to have two types: repeating and non-repeating
  • A repeating FRB are observed to repeat
  • However, a non-repeating FRB could potentially be an actual repeater but its repetition is not yet observed.

Meaning

‘Unsupervised Machine Learning’

  • Unsupervised machine learning is a type of machine learning algorithm that does not rely on labels (True/False or ‘repeating’/‘non-repeating’).

Important

The machine learning algorithms presented in this paper only processes the FRB features’ values (such as dispersion measure and redshift) and not the labels (repeating vs non-repeating).

Meaning

‘Unsupervised Machine Learning’

Examples

  • Dimension Reduction: A task that aims to reduce high dimensions of data into lower dimensions for better understanding of their relationships.
  • Clustering: A task that aims to categorizes data points into clusters based on their distance.

Objective

  • Use dimensional reduction algorithm and clustering on the reduced dimension to identify possibly misclassified non-repeating FRBs. These ‘possibly misclassified non-repeating FRBs’ are referred to as ‘repeater candidates’.

Results

Dimension Reduction (UMAP)

Highlights

  1. The distribution of repeating and non-repeating FRBs are similar (no sign of over-fitting).
  2. Repeaters and non-repeaters are concentrated within their respective clusters.
  3. There are non-repeaters mixed in repeaters–concentrated clusters.

Results

Clustering (HDBSCAN)

Highlights

  1. The HDBSCAN found 9 clusters of FRB samples.
  2. 3 of them are assigned as repeater clusters. A cluster is assigned as a repeater cluster if it is populated with >10% repeater FRB.
  3. Non-repeaters inside a repeater cluster are identified as repeater candidate. A total of 118/474 (39.7%) are identified as a repeater candidates.
  4. Total repeater percentages might be higher (41.9%) than previously thought (~5%).

Implications

Highest Frequency vs Peak Frequency

Highlights

  1. Repeaters (❌) are already aligned along the diagonal.
  2. Repeater candidates (♦️) also align along the diagonal.
  3. Non-repeaters (🟦) concentrate below the diagonal.

Implications

Highest/Peak Frequency vs Fluence

Highlights

  1. The line \(x = 1.4\) neatly separate repeater clusters and non-repeater clusters.
  2. All repeaters (❌) and repeater candidates (♦️) are located at the ratio \(x<1.4\).
  3. Most non-repeaters (🟦) are located at the ratio \(x>=1.4\) while some of them bleed through \(x<1.4\).
  4. Some FRBs form a straight line at \(\text{Fluence}=50\,\text{Jy}\cdot\text{ms}\) but the cause is unknown.

The ratio between the highest and peak frequencies is used because the redshift effect on these two measurements is cancelled out.

Implications

Width vs Spectral Running

Highlights

  1. The distribution of spectral running appears to have two main peaks: an upper peak around 0 ~ -10 and a lower peak between 0 and -150.
  2. A simple cut at ‘-25’, as shown by the dashed line, can divide them effectively into two major peaks.
  3. Repeaters (❌) and repeater candidates (♦️) make up the lower distribution.
  4. Non-repeaters (🟦) make up the upper distribution.

Conclusion

  1. Unsupervised machine learning can tell the difference between repeaters and non-repeaters even though it has no knowledge of the labels. Therefore, repeaters and non-repeaters may have an underlying difference; including a difference in origin.
  2. Some non-repeating FRBs have latent repeater features which is revealed using UMAP–HDBSCAN. Different plots have revealed that the repeater candidates shows similar behaviour with known repeaters.

Reproducibility
